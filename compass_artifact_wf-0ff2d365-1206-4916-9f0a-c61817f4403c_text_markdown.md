# Building the definitive resource on moral psychology, AI ethics, and education

**This report synthesizes the most current research (2023–2026) across five interconnected domains** — moral psychology, AI ethics in education, philosophical thought experiments, philosophy of education, and key voices — to serve as the foundation for an authoritative web resource. The central finding across all domains is convergent: the process of learning, moral reasoning, and human development cannot be reduced to information transfer, and AI's rapidly expanding capabilities do not resolve this fundamental truth. What follows is a detailed, citation-rich compendium organized by domain.

## Part 1: Moral psychology under pressure (2023–2026)

### Greene's dual-process theory faces a reckoning

Joshua Greene's influential dual-process model of moral judgment — which posits that deontological judgments arise from fast emotional responses while utilitarian judgments emerge from slower cognitive deliberation — is under significant empirical and philosophical pressure. Greene himself acknowledged this in his **2023 *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* commentary** ("Dual-process moral judgment beyond fast and slow," *BBS*, 46, e123, DOI: 10.1017/S0140525X22003193), responding to Wim De Neys's target article "Advancing theorizing about fast-and-slow thinking" (*BBS*, 46, e111). Greene conceded that De Neys makes "a compelling case that the sacrificial moral dilemmas do not elicit competing 'fast and slow' processes," but asked: "Are there even two processes? Or just two intuitions?" He defended the core claim using neuropsychological evidence from vmPFC lesion studies, concluding: **"The dual-process theory gets much right, but needs revision."**

The BBS exchange generated **34+ commentaries** from leading theorists. Keith Frankish proposed moving "Toward dual-process theory 3.0" (e122). Keith Stanovich and Maggie Toplak agreed that "exclusivity is exclusively in the past" (e142). David Melnikoff and John Bargh argued more radically that "the systems do not exist." De Neys synthesized responses in "Further advancing fast-and-slow theorizing" (e384).

Greene's recent research has partly shifted toward political psychology and intergroup conflict. Notable 2024–2025 publications include Caviola and Greene, "Moral psychology and utilitarianism" (in Chappell, Meissner, & MacAskill, eds., *An Introduction to Utilitarianism*, utilitarianism.net, 2024); Woodley, DeFilippis, Ravi, and Greene, "Online quiz game builds bridges between members of opposite parties" (*Nature Human Behaviour*, 2025); and a **megastudy testing 25 treatments to reduce anti-democratic attitudes** (Voelkel et al., *Science*, 386, 290).

### Bammel's modularity critique and the Cosmides challenge

**Moritz Bammel** (Leuphana University Lüneburg) published a meta-theoretical critique, "Greene's dual-process moral psychology and the modularity of mind" (*Philosophical Psychology*, published online December 20, 2024, DOI: 10.1080/09515089.2024.2444503, Open Access). Bammel argues that Greene's theory **implicitly relies on a modular account of cognition** (à la Fodor, 1983), positing two anatomically and functionally dissociable cognitive modules. Dynamical systems approaches in cognitive neuroscience reject strict modularity, conceiving the brain as a nonlinear system with constant plasticity. If we adopt this perspective, Greene's original theory — positing "two asymmetrically independent processes" — is no longer tenable. Only non-exclusive dual-process theories survive. A related paper by Gim (2025), "A Unified Bayesian Model of Moral Judgment: From Greene's Dual-Process to Predictive Coding" (*Philosophy, Thought, Culture*, 48(2), 1–25), offers a Bayesian alternative.

The **Cosmides challenge** came via Guzmán, Barbato, Sznycer, and Cosmides (2022), "A moral trade-off system produces intuitive judgments that are rational and coherent" (*PNAS*, 119(42), e2214005119). Using trolley-like dilemmas where participants could choose intermediate options (not just binary deontological/utilitarian), they found that **compromise moral judgments satisfy the generalized axiom of revealed preferences (GARP)** — demonstrating rational, coherent moral intuitions. They proposed a "moral tradeoff system" (MTS) constructing "rightness functions." Greene responded in *PNAS* (2023, 120(6), e2220396120), arguing his model was mischaracterized. Cosmides et al. replied (2023, *PNAS*, 120(24), e2220909120): "No version of the dual process model can explain rational performance by people who made compromise moral judgments."

### The meta-analytic verdict is sobering

A **2024 meta-analysis** in *Frontiers in Psychology* (15, 1388966) tested Greene's model using cognitive load, ego depletion, induction, and time restriction manipulations across **44 studies, 68 effect sizes, and 14,003 participants**. The overall pooled effect was in the predicted direction but **did not reach statistical significance**. Among peer-reviewed studies, a small significant effect emerged (g = 0.08, 95% CI [0.01, 0.14], p = 0.023), but gray literature effects ran in the opposite direction (g = −0.05, n.s.), suggesting **publication bias**. Earlier work by Rosas and Aguilar-Pardo (2020, *Thinking and Reasoning*, 26(4), 534–551) found extreme time pressure *increased* utilitarian responses — opposite to dual-process predictions. A Japanese replication (2025) similarly found utilitarian judgments *decreased* with deliberation.

### New neuroimaging studies (2024–2026)

Moral neuroscience continues advancing, increasingly moving beyond trolley paradigms:

- **Xu and Wu (2025)**, "Unraveling the neural basis of repeated moral decisions with mouse tracking and fMRI" (*Imaging Neuroscience*, MIT Press, DOI: 10.1162/IMAG.a.1047) — found that the **precuneus** stably encodes choice conflict, with PCC-IFG connectivity predicting individual differences in self-consistency versus reward weighting.
- **Caspar, Rovai, Lo Bue, and Cleeremans (2025)**, "Neural correlates of the sense of agency in free and coerced moral decision-making among civilians and military personnel" (*Cerebral Cortex*, 35(3), bhaf049) — compared civilians and military cadets, finding military training modulates reliance on cognitive versus emotional subsystems.
- **Farmani, Sharifi, and Ghazizadeh (2024)**, "Robust memory of face moral values is encoded in the human caudate tail" (*Scientific Reports*, 14, 63085) — demonstrated that moral value memories for faces are encoded **subcortically** in the caudate tail, not cortical areas.
- A **2025 *bioRxiv* preprint** identified a "Four-dimensional neural space for moral inference" — virtue, vice, hierarchy, and rebellion — with the **temporoparietal junction** activated across all moral dimensions during naturalistic film viewing.
- A *Frontiers in Psychology* study (2025, 16, 1451590) used cognitive load to test the Dual Process Model 2.0 for intentionality attribution, finding cognitive load reduced positive-side-effect intentionality attributions consistent with System 2 involvement.

### The trolley problem evolves beyond the tracks

Trolley problem research has shifted primarily toward autonomous vehicle (AV) ethics, but with growing skepticism about the paradigm's applicability:

- **Cecchini, Brantley, and Dubljević (2025)**, "Moral judgment in realistic traffic scenarios: moving beyond the trolley paradigm" (*AI & Society*, 40, 1037–1048) — argued that the trolley dilemma excludes character-based considerations and cannot address low-stakes mundane scenarios, proposing the Agent-Deed-Consequences (ADC) model instead.
- A **2024 *World Electric Vehicle Journal*** analysis (15(9), 404) demonstrated that the "default loss assumption" required for the trolley problem does not hold in autonomous driving design.
- A **2025 ERP study** (*Scientific Reports*, 15, 16048) expanded the Moral Machine experiment with EEG, finding increased P3 and late positive potential amplitudes when AI decisions were incongruent with participants' moral preferences.
- An **expanded Moral Machine survey** (2024, *Ethics and Information Technology*, PMC 11078731) showed that people's moral preferences **shift rapidly with minor framing changes**, cautioning against using simplified dilemmas for AV policy.

### Moral psychology meets AI alignment

The most significant bridge between moral psychology and AI safety is **Millière (2025)**, "Normative conflicts and shallow AI alignment" (*Philosophical Studies*, DOI: 10.1007/s11098-025-02347-3). Drawing on dual-process theories, Millière argues that current LLM alignment methods "reinforce shallow behavioral dispositions rather than endowing LLMs with a genuine capacity for normative deliberation." Humans' Type 2 deliberative reasoning makes them resilient to adversarial manipulation of moral commitments; **LLMs lack this capacity**, resulting in "shallow alignment" vulnerable to jailbreaking. Even reasoning-focused models (o1, DeepSeek R1) have not solved this. A related Chinese paper (2025, *Journal of Psychological Science*, Beijing Normal University) proposes "dual-system moral architectures" emulating interaction between intuitive and deliberative reasoning for AI alignment.

A **NeurIPS 2023 workshop**, "AI meets Moral Philosophy and Moral Psychology," brought together psychologists, philosophers, and AI researchers. A 2025 paper on "Moral disagreement and the limits of AI value alignment" (PMC 12628449) challenged the assumption that aggregating crowdsourced moral judgments can produce reliable AI alignment, arguing they fail both epistemic reliability and democratic legitimacy conditions.

### Haidt's pivot to technology and youth

Jonathan Haidt's **2024 book *The Anxious Generation*** (Penguin Press) argues smartphones and social media have "rewired" childhood since the late 2000s, causing an epidemic of anxiety, depression, and self-harm among Gen Z. He identifies four harms (social deprivation, sleep deprivation, attention fragmentation, addiction) and proposes four norms: no smartphones before 14, no social media before 16, phone-free schools, and more real-world independence. The book was a **#1 *NYT* bestseller for 52+ consecutive weeks** and catalyzed legislation including Australia's Online Safety Amendment (2025). Critics including Candice Odgers (*Nature*, 2024) argued the evidence is insufficient. Haidt's Moral Foundations Theory (five+ foundations) continues to be developed by collaborators, though his own focus has shifted. A follow-up book, *Life After Babel*, was due in early 2025.

## Part 2: AI ethics in education enters an evidence-based era

### UNESCO's 2025 framework crystallizes

UNESCO released two major reports at **Digital Learning Week 2025** (September 2–5, Paris):

**"AI and the Future of Education: Disruptions, Dilemmas and Directions"** (September 2025, 160+ pages) identifies seven areas for action, from defining AI futures to tackling inequality. It argues AI adoption should be a deliberate choice, not treated as inevitable. Teachers are called "the backbone of education," and the report warns against hyper-personalization and excessive automation, stressing that **"AI must recognize the incomputable nature of human learning."** Wayne Holmes cautioned: "Until we know, we're effectively experimenting on children."

**"AI and Education: Protecting the Rights of Learners"** (September 4, 2025, UNESDOC: ark:/48223/pf0000395373) adopts a human-rights-based approach using UNESCO's 5C framework. It notes that **~2.6 billion people still lack internet access** and only 40% of primary schools globally have internet, emphasizing that without safeguards — data protection, ethical frameworks, transparent governance — the right to education is at risk.

UNESCO's **AI Competency Framework for Teachers** (September 2024, UNESDOC: ark:/48223/pf0000391104), authored by Fengchun Miao and Mutlu Cukurova (UCL), defines **15 competencies across 5 dimensions** (human-centred mindset, ethics of AI, AI foundations, AI pedagogy, professional development) with three progression levels (Acquire → Deepen → Create). This was the **first-ever global AI competency framework for teachers**. A parallel **AI Competency Framework for Students** (September 2024, UNESDOC: ark:/48223/pf0000391105) covers 4 domains with similar progression levels.

### School districts build governance infrastructure

**NYC Public Schools** (March 24, 2026) released the most detailed U.S. district AI policy, using a **traffic light framework**: Red (prohibited — no AI-driven decisions on placement, discipline, graduation, or IEPs; no student surveillance), Yellow (conditional use with educator judgment), Green (approved uses). All AI tools must pass a 10-step data privacy review (ERMA). The policy was built through 25 rounds of feedback with 1,000+ stakeholders.

**LAUSD** (updated January 2026) introduced AI literacy curriculum in grades 6–12 but adopted a cautious stance after its AI chatbot vendor AllHere collapsed. **Chicago Public Schools** designated 2024–25 as a "learning year" with full integration planned for 2025–26. At the state level, **Ohio** (House Bill 96, August 2025) mandated formal AI policies for every public district by July 1, 2026. **Tennessee** (March 2024) became the first state to legally require all districts to publicly post AI use policies. By late 2025, **33+ states** had official AI guidance.

The **April 2025 Executive Order** "Advancing Artificial Intelligence Education for American Youth" established a White House Task Force and launched the "Pledge to America's Youth," with 200+ tech companies committing to K-12 AI education resources.

### What does the evidence say about AI in classrooms?

A **2025 meta-analysis** in *Educational Research Review* (68 studies, 337 effect sizes, PRISMA guidelines) found a **moderate positive overall effect** (SMD = 0.45, 95% CI [0.43, 0.47]) for GenAI-supported interventions, but effects vary significantly by discipline, level, and instructional context. However, a **Microsoft Research report** (Walker & Vorvoreanu, October 2025) warned that "productivity gains from GenAI ≠ learning gains." High school math students scored **17% lower** after using GenAI (Bastani et al., 2024). Students became overconfident about mastery, and GenAI impaired memory formation when used without scaffolding. A systematic review by Hon (2026, *Journal of Educational Technology Systems*, SAGE) confirmed **mixed evidence**: increased engagement in some contexts but over-reliance and variable effectiveness in others.

### AI detection remains fundamentally unreliable

Multiple studies confirm that AI detection tools have significant structural limitations. The **JISC 2025 assessment** found mainstream paid tools (Turnitin, CopyLeaks) report false positive rates of ~1–2% for unmodified AI text, but **paraphrasing reduces detection accuracy by >20%**. A Stanford study by **Liang et al. (2023, *Patterns*)** found detectors **misclassified 61% of non-native English writing as AI-generated**. ESL submissions were up to 30% more likely to be falsely flagged in 2024 follow-up studies. Neurodivergent students (autism, ADHD, dyslexia) face disproportionate flagging. At least **12 major universities** (Yale, Johns Hopkins, Vanderbilt, Waterloo) have disabled Turnitin's AI detection. Education Week reported that Black students are more likely to be falsely accused.

### Student AI use is near-universal

The **HEPI Student Generative AI Survey 2025** found **92% of UK undergraduates** use AI tools (up from 66% in 2024), with 88% using GenAI for assessment preparation (up from 53%). The **Digital Education Council Global Survey** (2024) reported **86% of students globally** use AI in studies, with 54% using it weekly. The **Inside Higher Ed/Generation Lab survey** (July 2025, 1,047 students, 166 institutions) found top reasons for policy-violating AI use: grade pressure (37%), time pressure (27%), and indifference to policies (26%). A **RAND study** (December 2025) found student AI homework use grew from 48% to **62%** in seven months, with 60% expressing concern about AI's impact on critical thinking.

### The EU AI Act makes education high-risk

The **EU AI Act** (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, in force August 1, 2024) classifies education as a **high-risk domain** under Annex III. Four specific use cases are regulated: AI systems determining access/admission to educational institutions, evaluating learning outcomes, assessing appropriate education level, and monitoring/detecting prohibited behavior during tests. Requirements include risk management, data governance, human oversight, and accuracy standards, with **fines up to €35 million or 7% of global turnover**. Critically, the Act **bans emotion recognition systems** in educational institutions (effective February 2, 2025), classified as "unacceptable risk." Article 4 mandates AI literacy for all staff deploying AI systems.

Saarela, Gunaserka, and Karimov (2025, "The EU AI Act: Implications for Ethical AI in Education," *DESRIST 2025*, Springer LNCS, pp. 36–50) argue the Act converts earlier ethical aspirations into enforceable obligations, establishing education as a "special-protection domain" similar to healthcare.

## Part 3: Thought experiments converge on a single insight

### Nozick's Experience Machine gains new life in the AI age

Robert Nozick's 1974 thought experiment (*Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, pp. 42–45) — would you plug into a machine simulating any pleasurable experience? — has been revitalized by AI companion technologies. A **2024 paper** in *Annals of the University of Bucharest* ("Re-Examining Nozick's Experience Machine in View of Emerging AI Companions") modifies the original scenario for shorter durations and preserved memory, finding what matters is not what people *say* they would choose but what they **actually choose** — highlighting the gap between stated preferences and behavior. Hindriks and Douven (2019, *Philosophical Psychology*, 32(2)) empirically tested multiple variations, finding that the less invasive the intervention, the more willing people were to accept it. Experimental philosophy work by **De Brigard (2010)** and **Weijers (2014)** challenged Nozick by showing responses are confounded by **status quo bias** rather than genuine preference for reality.

For education: if AI tutoring systems provide simulated mastery experiences, does the "experience" of learning have the same value as real-world engagement? Nozick's argument suggests **authentic engagement with reality** matters for human flourishing beyond subjective experience.

### Mary's Room reveals what propositional knowledge cannot deliver

Frank Jackson's Knowledge Argument ("Epiphenomenal Qualia," *Philosophical Quarterly*, 1982; "What Mary Didn't Know," *Journal of Philosophy*, 1986) has been directly applied to education by **Brock and Hay** (2019, "Keeping Students Out of Mary's (Class)room," *Science & Education*, 28, 985–1000, Springer Open Access). They argue that students learning scientific concepts without direct experience are like Mary — possessing propositional knowledge but lacking phenomenal understanding. They advocate a "phenomenological curriculum" ensuring **direct sensory experience alongside theoretical knowledge**. On the AI side, LessWrong analyses have drawn parallels between Mary and reinforcement learning agents, suggesting AI may process all physical information yet lack experiential understanding.

### Plato's Cave frames AI outputs as shadows

Plato's *Republic*, Book VII (514a–520a) has been applied to AI literacy by **Waitzman (2025)**, "Beyond Shadows: Plato's Cave, Artificial Intelligence, and the Challenge of Critical Thinking in Schools" (SSRN), which develops a **Framework for Critical AI Literacy**. Waitzman positions teachers as philosophical guides helping learners interrogate AI-generated "shadows" through a four-stage pedagogical cycle: Exposure → Interrogation → Comparison → Reflection. The allegory frames education as transformative reorientation — **the process of "turning the soul" cannot be shortcut** by information transfer, and AI outputs risk being mistaken for genuine understanding.

### Searle's Chinese Room challenges AI tutoring's foundations

John Searle's argument ("Minds, Brains, and Programs," *BBS*, 3(3), 1980, pp. 417–424) has gained renewed relevance with LLMs in education. A January 2025 paper in *Inquiry* ("LLMs, Turing Tests and Chinese Rooms") argued LLM outputs should be viewed as "genuinely meaningful" even without original intentionality, distinguishing meaning *per se* from conscious understanding. The **Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy** substantially revised its Chinese Room entry in October 2024. For education: if an AI tutor **manipulates symbols without understanding**, can it genuinely teach? The distinction between syntactic processing and semantic understanding parallels the difference between surface learning and deep comprehension.

### Lemire's Learning Pill anticipates the AI debate

Computer scientist **Daniel Lemire** (Université du Québec/TELUQ) published "The learning pill" on November 16, 2012 (lemire.me/blog), proposing: imagine a pill granting instant expert knowledge, free and side-effect-free. He predicted: the pill would be **made illegal**, educators would argue knowledge is "just one component of education," degree requirements would persist because they signal character, and economic/political resistance would protect existing institutions. His conclusion: **even a perfect learning pill would not disrupt colleges**, because education serves social, signaling, and character-building functions beyond knowledge transfer. This directly anticipates arguments about AI in education. His related 2025 post "We see something that works, and then we understand it" argued against "thinkism" — the belief that understanding precedes practice.

### The Matrix and Brave New World round out the canon

The *Matrix* (1999) "I know kung fu" scene — where Neo receives instant martial arts downloads but still requires training with Morpheus — illustrates that **downloaded knowledge requires practice, mentorship, and belief** to become functional. The British Educational Research Association used this to argue "the true potential of the Metaverse lies not in accelerated efficiency of knowledge transfer but in revitalising what education can be." Huxley's *Brave New World* (1932) hypnopaedia works only for conditioning attitudes, not teaching factual knowledge — distinguishing indoctrination from education.

### Dewey and Aristotle provide the philosophical bedrock

**John Dewey** (*Democracy and Education*, 1916; *Experience and Education*, 1938) argued education is not preparation for life but life itself. His two key principles — **continuity** (connecting past, present, and future experiences) and **interaction** (active engagement between learner and environment) — both require a temporal process that cannot be collapsed into an instant. "There is an intimate and necessary relation between the process of actual experience and education." The teacher is a guide, not a transmitter. If AI tutoring systems merely transmit information, however efficiently, they reproduce the "traditional" model Dewey criticized.

**Aristotle** (*Nicomachean Ethics*, Book II, 1103a–1105b) argued that virtue arises through habituation (*ethos*): "For the things we have to learn before we can do them, we learn by doing them — men become builders by building and lyre players by playing the lyre." His concept of *hexis* (active disposition) is not passive habit but an **active condition requiring ongoing engagement**. Crucially, Aristotle critiqued those who "take refuge in arguments, thinking they are doing philosophy" — directly rejecting the idea that knowing the good is sufficient for being good. For AI: character cannot be downloaded, and skipping the process of learning would be like claiming to be a lyre player without ever touching a lyre.

All eight thought experiments converge: **the process of learning is constitutive of its value, not merely instrumental to it.**

## Part 4: Philosophy of education confronts the automation question

### "Bullshit Universities" — the sharpest critique yet

**Sparrow and Flenady (2025)**, "Bullshit Universities: The Future of Automated Education" (*AI & Society*, 40, 5285–5296, Open Access, Springer) make three central claims: (1) Drawing on Harry Frankfurt's concept of "bullshit" — content produced without concern for truth — it would be misguided to replace teachers with AI that generates text without understanding; (2) The history of automation in other industries shows it is naïve to think AI can assist teachers without eventually replacing them, because **"money talks"** — economic pressures will push institutions to cut costs; (3) The dream of AI teaching neglects three essential features: the importance of "learning how" for "learning that," teachers as role models, and the social nature of education. This paper explicitly invokes the is/ought distinction, acknowledging it *is possible* for computers to replace teachers but arguing they *ought not*.

### Biesta's three purposes define what AI cannot deliver

**Gert Biesta's** framework (2010, 2014, 2020, 2022, 2024) identifies three purposes of education: **qualification** (knowledge and skills), **socialization** (communicating norms and values), and **subjectification** (becoming an autonomous subject of one's own life). Biesta argues that if education serves only qualification and socialization, it risks becoming training or indoctrination. **Subjectification** — involving freedom, ethical agency, and the "beautiful risk" of education — requires interruption, existential confrontation, and irreducibly human encounter. AI cannot provide subjectification because it cannot challenge a student's "egocentric orientation" through authentic ethical encounter.

A 2024/2025 paper applying Biesta's framework to GenAI argues that AI creates a **"placebo effect"** (the illusion of subjectification without authentic effort) and a **"nocebo effect"** (students' retreat from becoming autonomous subjects due to offloaded responsibility). Coelho et al. (2025, *British Educational Research Journal*) offers a two-level framework for operationalizing Biesta's purposes, noting subjectification is the "most misunderstood" yet foundational dimension.

### What students lose when learning is automated

**Hasan (2025)** ("How AI Quietly Undermines the Joy and Effort of Learning," *Annals of Medicine and Surgery*, 87(8), 4693–4694) provides neuroscientific grounding: AI tools that offer immediate solutions **bypass the brain's effort-reward cycle** — the dopamine-mediated process where overcoming intellectual challenges reinforces motivation and engagement. **Pondiscio (2025)**, writing for the American Enterprise Institute, frames it memorably: "Education is not a product to be delivered; it's a transformation that occurs through effort. The problem with AI is that it can perform education's outputs — essays, analyses, answers — **without any of its inputs**."

The research on **desirable difficulties** (Bjork & Bjork, 2011, *Psychology and the Real World*) and **productive failure** (Kapur, 2024, *Productive Failure: Unlocking Deeper Learning Through the Science of Failing*, Wiley) demonstrates why friction matters. Learning conditions that reduce performance during practice enhance long-term retention and transfer. Kapur's "4 As" framework (Activation, Awareness, Affect, Assembly) maps how carefully designed failure produces deeper conceptual understanding. **Bellwether Education Partners (2025)** synthesized this evidence in "Productive Struggle: How AI Is Changing Learning, Effort, and Youth Development," noting that AI's default tendency is to eliminate struggle entirely, threatening the cognitive mechanisms that produce genuine learning.

### Key philosophers of technology address education

**Shannon Vallor** (*The AI Mirror*, Oxford UP, 2024; shortlisted for Royal Institute of Philosophy prize) argues AI is a "recursive epistemic mechanism" distorting self-understanding, and that AI lacks *phrónēsis* (Aristotelian practical wisdom) for adapting ethical principles to complex circumstances. **Luciano Floridi** (*The Ethics of Artificial Intelligence*, Oxford UP, 2023) frames AI as a new form of *agency*, not intelligence — education cultivates human agency, not replaces it. **Mark Coeckelbergh** (*AI Ethics*, MIT Press, 2020; *Why AI Undermines Democracy*, 2024) addresses the power and governance dimensions. **Neil Selwyn** (*Should Robots Replace Teachers?*, Polity, 2019) established the template for distinguishing descriptive from normative claims about AI in education — praised by Frank Pasquale as "the gold standard."

## Part 5: The evolving landscape of key voices (2024–2026)

### Sal Khan's optimism meets reality

In a landmark **Chalkbeat interview** (April 9, 2026), Khan candidly admitted: "For a lot of students, it was a non-event. They just didn't use it much." **Khanmigo** grew from 40,000 pilot users to 700,000 in 2024–25, but Khan acknowledged AI tutoring doesn't solve motivation or fill the knowledge gaps needed to ask good questions. Khan Academy overhauled its product to integrate Khanmigo directly into practice exercises after finding **~80–85% of students didn't know how to initiate questions** with a standalone chatbot. Khan remains optimistic but more measured, advocating AI as enhancing "HI — human intelligence, human potential and human purpose" while stressing guardrails and transparency. A major Ohio partnership (August 2025) unlocked all Khan Academy tools for free across the state.

### Suleyman articulates "humanist superintelligence"

**Mustafa Suleyman**, appointed CEO of Microsoft AI in March 2024, published "Towards Humanist Superintelligence" (November 6, 2025, microsoft.ai), rejecting "narratives about a race to AGI" and proposing AI that remains "controllable, aligned, and firmly in service to humanity." On education specifically, he predicted "AI Companions will have a profound impact on how we learn...tailored learning methods, adaptive curricula, completely customized exercises. 'One size fits all' education will seem as bizarre to the next generation as rote learning Latin does to us." Microsoft's **Copilot "Learn Live" feature** (October 2025) launched a voice-enabled Socratic tutor with interactive whiteboards.

### Ethan Mollick emerges as the field's most influential voice

**Ethan Mollick** (Wharton, University of Pennsylvania) has become arguably the most influential voice on AI in education through his *New York Times* bestselling book *Co-Intelligence: Living and Working with AI* (April 2024, 19 languages), his widely read Substack "One Useful Thing" (**345K Twitter/X followers**), and his role as Co-Director of Wharton's Generative AI Labs. Named to TIME's Most Influential People in AI list, Mollick advocates treating AI as "co-intelligence" — co-worker, co-teacher, and coach — and urges educators to actively engage rather than ignore AI. His 2025 research paper "AI Agents and Education: Simulated Practice at Scale" explores AI-enabled practice environments.

### Rose Luckin warns of cognitive erosion

**Rose Luckin** (UCL Professor Emerita, founder of Educate Ventures Research, co-founder of the Institute for Ethical AI in Education) publishes the influential monthly newsletter "The Skinny on AI for Education." In her February 2026 issue, she warned of a **"Jevons Paradox for cognitive effort"** — AI adoption risks eroding sustained attention, critical judgment, and deep thinking. She highlighted McKinsey's finding that **88% use AI but only 6% get meaningful returns** and identified five paradoxes: centralization vs. democratization, speed vs. safety, massive investment vs. unclear returns, capability vs. evaluation, and growing power vs. fragile trust.

### Priya Lakhani delivers the defining TED talk

**Priya Lakhani** (CEO of CENTURY Tech, co-founder of the Institute for Ethical AI in Education, OBE) delivered "This is how kids should be learning with AI" at **TEDNext 2025** (November 10, Atlanta), arguing: "As AI enters classrooms, we risk confusing quick and easy answers with true learning. Being challenged is essential for making knowledge stick." Other notable 2025–2026 TED talks include Yoshua Bengio's sobering AI safety warning at TED2025 that AI planning capabilities are "doubling every seven months," and Priyanka Vergadia's "What you know that AI doesn't" (February 2026).

### Critical voices grow louder

The **Brookings Institution (2026)** concluded risks of AI to students outweigh benefits, citing cognitive offloading, dependency, and "banal deception" from anthropomorphic AI. The **Center for Democracy and Technology** (Laird, October 2025) found 85% of teachers and 86% of students used AI in 2024–25 but documented rising data breaches and tech-fueled harassment. A **RAND Corporation** study (2026) found 60% of AI-using students express concern about its impact on critical thinking. The **American Psychological Association** (June 2025) issued a health advisory warning AI companion software may "displace or interfere with development of healthy real-world relationships."

### Conferences and podcasts shaping discourse

Major gatherings include **ASU+GSV Summit** (April 2026, merging with The AI Show), **Stanford AI + Education Summit 2026** (co-hosted with Stanford HAI), and **MIT AI & Education Summit 2025**. Key podcasts include the **AI in Education Podcast** (Dan Bowen & Ray Fleming, weekly since 2019), the **Machine Ethics Podcast** (Ben Byford, 100+ episodes since 2015), **AI Ethics Now** (University of Warwick), and the **Ethics in AI Podcast** (Oxford's Institute for Ethics in AI). The **OECD** announced AI literacy will be assessed in the **2029 PISA** (MAIL test), with its AI Literacy Framework draft released May 2025.

## Conclusion: the irreducible process

Across all five domains, a unified insight emerges. Moral psychology shows that human moral reasoning involves distinct cognitive processes that cannot be replicated through surface-level alignment of AI systems — Millière's "shallow alignment" diagnosis connects Greene's dual-process theory directly to AI safety. Neuroimaging research increasingly reveals distributed, dynamic neural systems rather than discrete modules, challenging both strict dual-process models and the computational models that AI alignment researchers borrow from them.

In education, the evidence is simultaneously encouraging and cautionary: AI produces moderate positive effects on learning outcomes when used for knowledge construction, but **demonstrably impairs** memory formation, critical thinking, and intrinsic motivation when it bypasses productive struggle. Detection tools are unreliable and discriminatory. Nearly all students use AI, but most institutions lack coherent governance.

The philosophical tradition — from Plato through Aristotle, Dewey, Jackson, Nozick, Searle, and Lemire — converges on a single proposition: **knowledge is not separable from the process of acquiring it**. Nozick showed we want to *do* things, not just experience doing them. Mary's Room demonstrated that propositional knowledge cannot capture experiential understanding. Aristotle proved that virtue requires habituation through practice. Dewey established that education is life itself, not preparation for it. And Searle's Chinese Room reminds us that symbol manipulation without understanding is not cognition.

The most important insight for building an authoritative web resource is this: the debate is not about whether AI is useful in education (it demonstrably is, in specific contexts) but about whether the efficiency gains it offers are worth the developmental losses it risks. Sparrow and Flenady's warning that "money talks" — that economic pressures will push automation regardless of normative arguments — makes the philosophical case not merely academic but urgently practical. The task ahead is not to choose between AI and human education, but to ensure that the irreducible process of becoming a thinking, feeling, morally reasoning human being remains at the center of what education means.
